E2 project

Motivation and aims

An interesting and important ability of a neural tissue (especially of the cerebral cortex) is its adaptation to conditions in the environment in order to maximise (long term) options for its organism.

Although we have models for many important components and properties of neural tissue [3,2,1], e.g. models of neurons (in many different levels of details), synapses and synaptic plasticity, axon growth, layered structure of a micro column, etc., it is unknown what models of neural tissue model the adaptability with what accuracy. It is in particular unknown how the adaptability depends on model's properties like number of neurons in a micro column, number of neurons in whole model, in/out-degrees of neurons, distributions of excitatory and inhibitory synapses, lengths of axons, strength of attraction and repulsion cues for axon growth, numbers of sensory cells, number of output fibres (to models of muscles), small-world phenomenon, power low distribution of in/out-degrees of cells, and many others.

In this project we aim to fill this gap. Namely, we shall design, implement, and experimentally evaluate (simulate) many models of neural tissue constructed for different properties mentioned above in order to classify models according to their adaptability and to uncover influence of mentioned properties to the adaptability.

Of course, we cannot simulate models comparable in counts of neurons with the cerebral cortex of mammals or birds due to lack of computational power. Nevertheless, we may expect that the adaptability can be observed also in substantially smaller neural tissues (hundreds of thousands of neurons), when it has to adapt itself to conditions of substantially simpler environments.

We shall thus also design and implement several simple environments, in which individual agents, controlled by different models of neural tissue, harvest available resources. A key part of the design of an environment is a correlation between the adaptability to conditions of the environment and strategies for harvesting resources.

In summary, our endeavour is supposed to give us the following three important outputs (two theoretical and one practical):

  • Classification of models of neural tissue according to their adaptability to different environments.
  • Influence of properties of models of neural tissue to the adaptability.
  • Evaluation framework in which we shall perform all simulations.

First two outputs give us a good estimate how accurate the current models of neural tissue can be w.r.t. the adaptability to conditions of an environment. Moreover, this knowledge can be very useful for a researcher designing a new model: when it is known what properties are essential and which are not, the researcher may save o lot of time in construction of a desired model. Futher, a subsequent deep analysis of our outputs (e.g. why some properties are so important) may lead to more accurate and simultaneously efficient models.

The evaluation framework allows other researches to verify our results. Moreover, the previous two output can be easily extended in future using the framework, since it suffices to only implement a new model and/or an environment and plug it into the framework. Finally, the framework can be reused for study of different properties of neural tissue. We simply plug in different kinds of models and environments.

Although we now focus only on smaller models (due to limited available computational power), all three outputs can naturally be updated over time (by us and/or other researchers) for larger and larger models.

Methods and techniques

First of all we need a set of models of neural tissue to be examined. We shall design and implement these models for wide ranges of properties mentioned above. We shall also use different known models of components of neural tissue, like different models of neurons, synapses, axon growth, etc. Although we shall attempt to use models of different biological accuracy, our priority is simulation of larger models (up to hundreds of thousands of neurons). So, simpler models will be more preferable.

Besides models of neural tissue we further need models of environments. A model of an environment is a simple game with a single goal for agents: to collect resources. Agents are controlled by unique and independent models of neural tissue. Here are examples of possible environments (games):

                   

The game on the left is for one agent controlling the horisontal bar. Each time the bar hits the ball the agent receives a bit of energy (resource). The other two games are for more agents. The game in the middle requires cooperation of both agent in order to maximise resources' consumption. And the game on the right is competitive, where agents compete for resources (flowers).

A model of neural tissue receives information about the environment and all agents in it via sensory cells. Information about resources collected by its agent is delivered indirectly, by observation of capabilities of its agent (e.g. the agent can move faster when he has more energy).

All games are intentionally very similar in design and the goal. Models of neural tissue are supposed to show how they can adapt to their (slightly) different rules. We measure the adaptability of models in terms of collected resources by their agents.

The project has special requirements for the evaluation. We shall simulate models of many different sizes (from few hundreds of neurons up to hundreds of thousands of neurons per agent). Moreover, agent's model of neural tissue is actually just one component in a bigger model of whole the environment (game). We further require features like dynamical changes in connectivity between neurons, simulation of axon growth, or keeping of network properties like power law distribution of degrees of cells, etc. Therefore, although there are available software packages providing simulation of models of a neural tissue, e.g. [8,7,4,5,6], they are not suitable for our purposes. We thus implement a new evaluation framework which allows as to plug in many different models of neural tissue and also allowing as to set and maintain network properties like small-world phenomenon, power low distribution of in/out-degrees of cells, etc. Finally, the framework will further allow us to plug in different environments and interconnect agents with their models of neural tissue.
